What is the problem?

Tens of millions of EU citizens cannot verify whether their prescribed medicines align with their ethical, religious, or personal values. Not only patients — even medical professionals are unable to confirm species origin.

Since manufacturers are not required to provide origin information even in professional product documentation (e.g. SmPCs), pharmacists and clinicians cannot reliably advise patients or identify suitable alternatives.

Who is affected?

Vegans and vegetarians
Object to all animal-derived substances on ethical grounds.
Observant Muslims and Jews
Avoid porcine-derived ingredients on religious grounds.
Hindus
Avoid bovine-derived ingredients on religious grounds.
Patients with sensitivities
Patients with lactose intolerance or sensitivities to specific animal proteins face distinct medical constraints.
Medical professionals
Cannot reliably advise patients or identify suitable alternatives when origin is not documented in professional product information.

EU-level studies are lacking, but a 2022 study found that 74% of commonly prescribed medicines in the UK contained animal-derived components. Given shared pharmaceutical supply chains across the EU, a comparable pattern is likely reflected in every EU Member State.

Unlike food or cosmetics, medicines are frequently non-substitutable, leaving patients with no reliable means of making an informed choice at the point of prescription or dispensing.

Potential Solutions

Manufacturers already track excipient origin in the supply chain, so disclosure is mainly a labelling, not a formulation change — with minimal cost and no impact on medicine availability.

1. Simple labelling
Adding species origin to the already-existing ingredient list on packaging. Requires very little to no investment. Example: Magnesium stearate (animal-derived, bovine)
2. Extend description in SmPC
Adding species origin to the ingredient list in the Summary of Product Characteristics — the professional product documentation used by clinicians and pharmacists.
3. Centralised EU Database
An EU-level origin database linked to digital labelling, enabling patients and professionals to look up any medicine and see the species origin of all excipients.
Example labelling format:

Gelatine (animal-derived, porcine/bovine/fish)
Glycerol (plant-derived/synthetic or animal-derived, porcine/bovine/ovine/caprine)
Magnesium stearate (plant-derived/synthetic or animal-derived, porcine/bovine/ovine/caprine)
Lactose (animal-derived, bovine)
Shellac (animal-derived, insect)

Examples show the disclosure format, not a complete list.

Response from the European Commission


The European Commission confirms that a legislative gap exists and acknowledges that the issue affects millions of EU citizens. In a formal response dated 29 January 2026, signed by Olga Solomon, Head of Unit, Directorate-General for Health and Food Safety, the Commission stated:

The Commission's own words confirm that current law does not mandate origin disclosure, that the ethical case is recognised, and that the proposed reforms are under active consideration.

Key excerpts:

“Articles 54 and 59 of Directive 2001/83/EC do not necessitate the disclosure of the species origin of excipients, as the legislation is more focused on ensuring the safety, efficacy, and quality of medicinal products.”
“We recognise the importance of enabling patients to make informed choices in line with their ethical, cultural, or religious beliefs.”
“We value your suggestions for regulatory improvements, including potential amendments to Directive 2001/83/EC, or the introduction of measures such as a centralised database or standardised symbols.”

Frequently Asked Questions

Research indicates that most medicines use animal-derived excipients, including capsules, tablets, and coatings.
EU rules require excipient names to be listed, but they do not require species origin, so it often remains unclear.
Species origin can determine whether a medicine is acceptable for ethical, cultural, or religious reasons, while medicines are often unavoidable.
They typically document origin for supply-chain and quality-control purposes, so the main change is standardised disclosure.
Options include origin qualifiers in product information, a standardised symbol or notation on the box and/or Package leaflet, and an EU-level origin database linked to digital labelling.
