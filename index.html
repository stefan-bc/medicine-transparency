Why is non-disclosure a problem?

Animal ingredients in excipients Inactive ingredients such as capsule shells, fillers, and coatings. can create a serious ethical concern because their origin is often not disclosed by manufacturers.

Prevents informed consent
Animal species origin determines acceptability for many ethical and religious convictions, while medicines are often unavoidable.
Affects clinicians and pharmacists
Medical professionals cannot reliably answer origin questions from official product information or identify suitable alternatives when origin is obscured.
Creates indirect discrimination risk
Lack of origin disclosure disproportionately affects people whose ethical or religious convictions require avoiding certain animal-derived ingredients.

Solution

The EU can mandate labelling to show clearly when animal-derived products are used in medicines.
Manufacturers already track excipient origin in the supply chain, so disclosure is mainly a labelling, not a formulation change. Disclosure reduces uncertainty for patients and healthcare professionals without changing formulations, with minimal cost and no impact on medicine availability.
Example labelling:

Gelatine (animal-derived, porcine/bovine/fish)
Glycerol (plant-derived/synthetic or animal-derived, porcine/bovine/ovine/caprine)
Magnesium stearate (plant-derived/synthetic or animal-derived, porcine/bovine/ovine/caprine)
Lactose (animal-derived, bovine)
Shellac (animal-derived, insect)

Examples show the disclosure format, not a complete list.

Response from the European Commission

The Commission confirms the EU labelling gap on species origin for excipients in their letter.


Key excerpts:

“Articles 54 and 59 of Directive 2001/83/EC do not necessitate the disclosure of the species origin of excipients.”
“We recognise the importance of enabling patients to make informed choices in line with their ethical, cultural, or religious beliefs.”
“We will consider your points in discussions and studies related to improving transparency in medicinal labelling.”

Frequently Asked Questions

Research indicates that most medicines use animal-derived excipients, including capsules, tablets, and coatings.
EU rules require excipient names to be listed, but they do not require species origin, so it often remains unclear.
Species origin can determine whether a medicine is acceptable for ethical, cultural, or religious reasons, while medicines are often unavoidable.
They typically document origin for supply-chain and quality-control purposes, so the main change is standardised disclosure.
Options include origin qualifiers in product information, a standardised symbol or notation on the box and/or Package leaflet, and an EU-level origin database linked to digital labelling.
